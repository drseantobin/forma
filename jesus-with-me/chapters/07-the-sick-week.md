# Chapter Seven: The Sick Week

There was one winter when I got really, truly sick.

Not the kind of sick where you get to stay home and watch movies and it is secretly a little bit fun. The other kind. The kind where your whole body aches and the light hurts your eyes and you cannot even finish your soup.

My dad writes books, and one of his books is about being sick — it is called *The Sick Seven*. So when I got sick, he joked that I had become the eighth one. I did not laugh, because laughing hurt, but I did smile a tiny bit.

The days went by slow and blurry. I lost track of which day was which. I just stayed in my bed, under the heavy blanket, feeling small.

Being sick is lonely in a way I did not expect. Everyone is being nice to you, bringing you water and feeling your forehead, but you are still the only one who has to be inside the sick body. Nobody can do it for you. You are kind of alone in there.

One night, when I felt the very worst, my mom sat next to my bed and held my hand. I did not have the energy to talk. But she said something quietly.

"You know," she said, "when Jesus was here, he spent so much of his time with sick people. Everybody else stayed away from them, because they were afraid of catching something. But Jesus walked right up close. He was not scared of sick. He would put his hand right on people that nobody else would touch. He liked being near the ones who felt the worst."

I thought about that, lying there feeling like the worst.

"So he is not far away right now," I whispered. "Even though I am all gross and sick."

"Especially right now," my mom said. "This is exactly the kind of bedside he loves to sit at."

I closed my eyes. And I imagined him there, on the other side of the bed from my mom. Not scared of my fever. Not waiting for me to get better and prettier and less gross. Just sitting close in the dark, the way he had since I was four years old, the way he always has.

I got better that week. I always do. The soup started tasting good again, and the light stopped hurting, and one morning I woke up and I was just *me* again.

But I did not forget the sick week. Because that was the week I learned that Jesus is not only with me on my good days, when I am healthy and smiling at the baseball field in my gold shoes.

He is with me on my worst day. My grossest, achiest, soup-can't-finish day.

He sits at that bedside too.
