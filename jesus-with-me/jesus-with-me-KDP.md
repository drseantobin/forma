## Chapter One
# Learning With Jesus

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava sitting cross-legged on the floor, completely absorbed in a glowing open storybook, the boredom gone from her face.

Okay, so here is the first thing you need to know about me.

I do NOT love learning.

I mean, I like SOME learning. I like learning how to do a cartwheel. I like learning which candy is the best candy (it's the sour kind, I have done a lot of research). But the learning where you sit at a desk and somebody talks about long division for nine hundred hours? No thank you.

So when my parents first told me I was going to "learn about Jesus," my stomach did a little flip. I pictured a desk. I pictured a worksheet. I pictured raising my hand and getting the answer wrong in front of everybody.

But it turned out learning about Jesus is NOTHING like long division.

For one thing, nobody gives you a grade. There is no test. You cannot get an F in Jesus. (I checked. I asked my mom directly and she laughed for a really long time.)

For another thing, the stories are actually GOOD. Like, way better than the stories in my reading book about a dog named Spot. Jesus walked on top of water. He fed five thousand people with one kid's lunch. He made blind people see and turned regular water into a party drink. If you put that in a movie, I would watch it.

But here is the part that got me. The part I did not expect.

I always thought learning meant filling up your head with facts. Like stuffing socks in a drawer. The capital of France. Seven times eight. Boring stuff you memorize and then forget the second summer starts.

Learning about Jesus is not like stuffing socks in a drawer.

It is more like getting to know a person.

Because here is the secret nobody tells you at the start. When you learn ABOUT Jesus, you are really learning HIM. And the more I learned him, the more I figured out he was not a character in a book at all. He was a real person who is somehow ALSO right here, right now, in my actual room, while I am writing this.

That is a weird thing to wrap your brain around. I am still working on it. I will probably be working on it my whole life.

And you know what? For the first time ever, I do not mind the homework.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, I am not great at sitting still and learning. But I really do want to know you. Help me learn you a little more every day. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Learn from me, because I am gentle and humble."* (from Matthew 11:29)

## Chapter Two
# Praying When I Felt Down

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava lying face-down and flat on her bed in a gray-toned room, arms hanging off the sides — with the very first hint of warm light coming through the window.

Some days you just feel like a deflated balloon.

You know the feeling. Nothing even has to go WRONG, exactly. You just wake up gray. Like somebody snuck in during the night and let half the air out of you.

I had a day like that once. I am not even going to pretend there was a big dramatic reason, because there wasn't. It was just a gray day. My friend was a little short with me. I got the math problems wrong (again — see Chapter One). My hair would not do the thing I wanted it to do. Little stuff. But all the little stuff piled up into one big heavy feeling, and by the time I got home I just wanted to lie face-down on my bed and be a balloon.

My mom poked her head in and asked what was wrong. And I said the most honest thing I have ever said in my life:

"I don't even KNOW."

Because that is the worst kind of sad. The kind where you cannot even point at the reason.

My mom sat on the edge of my bed. She did not tell me to cheer up. (Thank goodness. When you are a deflated balloon and somebody says "cheer up!" you want to deflate EVEN MORE.) She just said, "Did you tell Jesus?"

And I said, "Tell him WHAT? I don't even have a reason."

And she said the thing I keep in my pocket now for gray days.

"You don't need a reason. You can just tell him you feel down. He's not waiting for you to explain it. He already knows. He just likes when you come to him with it instead of carrying it by yourself."

So that night, in the dark, I did not say a fancy prayer. I did not even sit up. I just whispered, "Jesus, I feel down today and I don't know why. Will you sit with me?"

And here is the honest truth, because this is an honest book.

I did not suddenly turn into a happy filled-up balloon. POOF. That is not how it works.

But I felt less alone with it. The gray was still gray. But I was not holding it by myself anymore. Somebody was sitting in the gray WITH me. And somehow that made the gray a lot easier to be in.

The next morning I woke up and most of the air was back. Funny how that happens.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, on my gray days, the ones I can't even explain, thank you that I don't have to carry the heavy feeling alone. Sit with me until the air comes back. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Come to me, all of you who are tired and carrying heavy things, and I will give you rest."* (from Matthew 11:28)

## Chapter Three
# Praying to Beat My Brother at Chess

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** The chessboard between Ava and her older brother. His side is full of grinning pieces; her side has one lonely, panicked little horse. A gentle, calm presence sits beside Ava.

Okay. I need to tell you about my brother.

He is two years older than me, which he thinks makes him the boss of the planet. And here is the really annoying part: he is GOOD at stuff. Especially chess.

I am not good at chess. I am the kind of good at chess where I lose my queen in about four moves and then spend the rest of the game just trying to keep my little horse guys alive.

My brother wins every single time. And he does this thing when he wins where he leans back and goes, "Good game," in a voice that makes me want to flip the whole board into outer space.

So one day before we played, I had a genius idea.

I would pray to WIN.

I figured, hey, Jesus can do anything, right? He walked on water. Beating my brother at chess should be easy for him. So I squeezed my eyes shut and prayed the most focused prayer of my whole life: "Dear Jesus, please, PLEASE let me beat my brother at chess just one time. Amen. Amen extra. Double amen."

Then we played.

And I lost.

In SIX moves.

He said, "Good game," and leaned back, and I had to use every ounce of my self-control not to launch the board into space.

That night I was kind of mad at Jesus, honestly. I prayed and everything! I did the double amen! What is the point of having an all-powerful friend if he won't even help you win at chess?

So I asked my dad about it.

And my dad said something that kind of blew my mind. He said, "Ava, Jesus isn't a vending machine. You don't put a prayer in and get a prize out. He's not there to help you WIN. He's there to be WITH you — whether you win or lose."

Then he said the part I really think about. "You prayed to beat your brother. But what if the better prayer is, 'Jesus, be with me whether I win or lose, and help me not flip the board'?"

So now I still pray before chess. But I changed the prayer. Now I pray, "Jesus, help me play my best, be a good sport, and stay with me even when I lose. (And okay, if you want to help me win ONE time, I would not say no.)"

I still haven't beaten him.

But I haven't flipped the board, either. And honestly? That might be the bigger miracle.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, you're not a machine that gives me prizes — you're my friend who stays with me. Help me play my best, lose like a good sport, and remember you're with me either way. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"My grace is enough for you, because my strength shows up best when you are weak."* (from 2 Corinthians 12:9)

## Chapter Four
# Praying When I Was Scared

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava's dark bedroom. Her coat on the hook looks like a tall, creepy monster — but Ava peeks out from her blanket, and a soft warm glow is starting to fill the room.

Let me tell you about the scariest thing in my whole house.

It is not the basement. It is not the spider that lives behind the toilet (although, honestly, that spider is huge and we have an understanding: he stays back there, I don't scream).

The scariest thing in my house is my bedroom at night, with the light off.

Here is the thing about the dark. Everything NORMAL turns into something TERRIBLE. In the daytime my coat is just a coat. At night it becomes a tall skinny guy standing very still in the corner, waiting. My backpack becomes a crouching creature. The little creak the house makes? Definitely footsteps. Definitely.

I used to do the thing where you pull the blanket all the way over your head, because everybody knows monsters cannot get you through a blanket. That is just science.

But you cannot breathe very well under a blanket, and also you get sweaty, so it is not a great long-term plan.

One night when I was really little, my dad told me a secret about the dark that I have used ever since. He said: "Ava, the dark is scary to YOU, but it is nothing to Jesus. He can see in the dark like it's the middle of the day. The dark doesn't hide anything from him. So when you're scared, you don't have to be brave all by yourself. You can just ask him to stay."

So I tried it.

I did not pull the blanket over my head. (Well, okay, I kept it at chin level. Halfway. Compromise.) And I whispered, "Jesus, you can see in the dark. Will you stay with me?"

And I want to be honest, because this is an honest book.

The dark did not turn into daytime. The light did not magically click on. The room was still dark.

But the SCARY part of the dark kind of melted. Because the dark only feels scary when you think you are alone in it. And I wasn't. The same person who can see in the dark was sitting right there with me, totally not scared, like the monster-coat was the silliest thing he had ever seen.

I am almost nine now. I am not really scared of the dark anymore. But I still say it some nights, just because I like knowing he's there.

"Jesus, will you stay with me?"

He always does.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, the dark can't hide anything from you. When I'm scared, remind me I'm not alone in it. Stay with me until I fall asleep. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Even the darkness is not dark to you; the night shines like the day."* (from Psalm 139:12)

## Chapter Five
# The Day I Was Mean (Not My Best Chapter)

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava, with a shaky-brave face, handing a kind word to Priya, whose drawing is back out on her desk. A small gentle glow behind them.

Okay, this chapter is embarrassing and I almost didn't put it in the book.

But my dad says the true stories are the ones worth telling, even the ones that make your face go hot. So. Deep breath. Here it is.

I was mean to a girl named Priya.

Here is what happened. Priya drew a picture in class, and I said something mean about it. Out loud. In front of everybody. The kind of mean thing you say just to get the other kids to laugh.

And it worked. They laughed.

For about one second, that felt great.

Then I looked at Priya's face. And her face had gone red, and her eyes had gone shiny, and she put her drawing inside her desk so nobody could see it anymore.

And just like that, the great feeling was GONE. It got replaced by the worst feeling in the world. The kind that sits in your stomach like a rock.

That night I couldn't sleep. The mean thing I said kept replaying in my head like an annoying song stuck on repeat. And I had the scariest thought of all: *Jesus probably saw that. He's probably so disappointed in me. He probably doesn't even want to be near me right now.*

So I told my mom. The whole thing. Even the part that made my face hot.

And my mom said something I will never, ever forget. She said: "Ava, Jesus didn't back away from you tonight. He came CLOSER. That terrible rock in your stomach? That's not him leaving. That's him helping you see you want to make it right. People who don't care feel nothing. You feel awful because your heart is good, and it knows it did a bad thing."

And then the best news in the whole world: "When you bring it to Jesus, he doesn't keep a list of your mess-ups. He forgives you. Totally clean. Like fresh snow. And then he helps you be braver tomorrow."

The next day was hard. My voice was shaky. But I went up to Priya and said I was sorry, and that her drawing was actually really good (which was TRUE). She didn't forgive me right away, and that was fair. But a few days later, she did.

And get this — now she's one of my actual friends.

Here is what I learned, and it might be the most important thing in this whole book:

Jesus doesn't only stick around when I'm being good. He stays even when I mess up bad. He stays close enough to help me fix it.

That's a friend who really, truly never leaves.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, I'm sorry for the times I'm unkind. Thank you that you don't keep a list, that you forgive me clean, and that you help me make things right. Make my heart more like yours. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"I don't condemn you. Go on now, and be kind."* (from John 8:11)

## Chapter Six
# Praying for Something I REALLY Wanted

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava holding an empty dog leash with nothing on the end, daydreaming of a brown puppy with one white ear (drawn in soft dotted lines) floating above her head.

So after the whole chess disaster (see Chapter Three), you'd think I'd have learned that Jesus is not a vending machine.

I had not fully learned it.

Because there was something I wanted SO BADLY that I decided to try praying for it anyway. And that something was: a dog.

I wanted a dog more than I have ever wanted anything. I had already named him. Waffles. I knew what color he'd be (brown, with one white ear). I knew he'd sleep on my bed even though my mom would say no and then secretly allow it.

So I launched Operation Pray For Waffles. Every single night: "Dear Jesus, please give me a dog. A brown one. With one white ear. Named Waffles. Thank you, amen."

I prayed for Waffles for a LONG time.

And here is what happened.

I did not get a dog.

At first I was bummed. Like, REALLY bummed. I thought maybe Jesus wasn't listening, or maybe I did the prayer wrong, or maybe I should've offered to do extra chores as a bribe.

But then something happened that I didn't expect. My grandma got sick, and she came to live with us for a while. And taking care of her took a LOT of time and a lot of room and a lot of quiet — and a brand-new puppy named Waffles would have been a tornado in the middle of all that.

And I got it. Not all the way. But a little.

My dad explained it like this: "Ava, sometimes the answer to a prayer is 'yes.' Sometimes it's 'not yet.' And sometimes it's 'I have something better in mind that you can't see yet.' Jesus isn't a vending machine where you push a button and get your snack. He's a friend who actually knows what's coming, and loves you too much to just hand you everything you ask for the second you ask."

So I learned that praying for stuff is totally allowed! You can ask Jesus for anything. He WANTS you to bring him what's in your heart.

But you have to trust that he can see the whole picture, and you can only see one corner of it.

I still want a dog. I'm not going to lie to you. Operation Waffles is on PAUSE, not CANCELED.

But I'm not mad anymore. Because I'm starting to figure out that "no" or "not yet" from Jesus isn't him ignoring me.

It's him loving me with the part of the story I can't see yet.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, I'm going to keep bringing you the things I want, because you said I could. But help me trust you when the answer is "not yet" or "I've got something better." You can see the whole story. I can only see one corner. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Which of you, if your kid asks for bread, would give them a rock? Your Father gives good gifts to those who ask."* (from Matthew 7:9–11)

## Chapter Seven
# Jesus at the Baseball Field

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava sitting up on the long, round yellow rail at the edge of the baseball field, feet dangling, gold sneakers catching the light, heart shirt on, a huge grin — the sun setting gold and pink behind the hills. *(This one would make a great cover.)*

Not everything in this book is about hard stuff and gray days. I don't want you to think I walk around being sad and deflated all the time. I am actually a pretty happy person!

So this chapter is about one of the best places in the whole world: the baseball field.

There's this field near us, and on summer evenings the sun goes down behind the hills and turns EVERYTHING gold. The grass goes gold. The dirt goes gold. Even the air kind of glows. It's the prettiest thing.

And there's this long yellow rail by the field — the round bumpy kind — and I love to climb up and sit on it with my feet dangling. From up there you can see the whole field, all the way to the mountains.

One evening I was up on that rail, in my heart shirt and my gold shoes that catch the light, and my whole family was there, and somebody was laughing, and the grass smelled like summer, and I had that feeling. You know the one. Where everything is just RIGHT and your heart gets so full it might tip over.

And out of nowhere, a thought just bubbled up out of me.

*Thank you.*

I didn't even mean to think it. It just came out of me, because the evening was so beautiful. And then I knew exactly who I was saying it to.

Because here's a thing I figured out: I used to think you only needed Jesus for the SCARY parts. The dark. The gray days. The chess losses. Like he was an umbrella you only grab when it rains.

But sitting up on that gold rail with my heart tipping over, I realized something better.

He's not just the umbrella for the rain.

He's the one who MADE the sunshine.

All of it — the gold light, the green grass, my family, the laughing, my heart so full it might tip over — all of it came FROM him. He didn't just make the hard days and then show up to help me through them. He made the GOOD days too. The golden ones. The best ones.

So now I try to remember to say thank you on the good days, not just to ask for help on the bad ones.

Because on the best evening of your life, when your heart is so full it might tip over, he's right there next to you on the rail, watching the sun go down.

Just as happy as you are.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, thank you for the golden days, the ones so good my heart could tip over. Help me remember to say thank you, not just to ask for help. You made the sunshine too. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"I came so you could have life, and have it FULL — all the way to the top."* (from John 10:10)

## Chapter Eight
# The Time I Asked Jesus to Help Me Not Strangle My Brother

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava and her brother on the floor together, rebuilding a magnificent block town — bigger castle, little block-dog back in place — both grinning. A warm glow over the whole scene.

Remember my brother from the chess chapter? The two-years-older boss-of-the-planet guy?

Well, this chapter is also about him. Because sometimes loving your brother and wanting to bonk him on the head with a pillow are the EXACT SAME FEELING at the EXACT SAME TIME.

Here's what happened. I built something. It took me a really long time. It was a whole little town made out of blocks — with a castle, and a bridge, and a tiny block dog (named Waffles, obviously — see Chapter Six).

And my brother walked through the living room and KNOCKED IT OVER. The whole thing. With his big dumb foot.

He SAID it was an accident. But he also did NOT say sorry, and he kind of half-smiled, and that half-smile made me want to launch HIM into outer space along with the chessboard from Chapter Three.

I was SO mad. The kind of mad where your face gets hot and your hands turn into fists and you want to say the meanest thing you can think of.

And then — I am not even kidding — a little voice in my head went: *"Remember Chapter Five? Remember being mean to Priya? Remember the rock in your stomach?"*

Ugh. I did remember.

So instead of saying the meanest thing I could think of, I stomped off to my room (stomping is allowed, stomping hurts no one), and I prayed the most honest prayer of my life:

"Jesus, I am SO mad at my brother right now. Please help me not strangle him. Also, he should say sorry. Also, I'm still mad. Amen."

And you know what? Jesus didn't make me NOT mad. Being mad isn't even a sin — Jesus got mad sometimes too! But while I sat there talking to him, the boiling part cooled down. The part where I wanted to be cruel. That part shrank.

And after a while my brother knocked on my door. And he said sorry. A REAL sorry. And he helped me rebuild the whole town, even better than before, with a bigger castle.

Here's what I figured out. When somebody makes you SO mad, you've got two doors you can walk through. Door one: say the cruel thing, win the fight, feel like a rock all night. Door two: take the mad feeling to Jesus FIRST, let him cool the boiling part, and then deal with it.

Door two is way harder.

But door two is how I got my brother back AND a bigger castle.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, when somebody makes me boiling mad, help me bring it to you before I do something I'll regret. Cool the cruel part. Help me forgive, even my brother. (Especially my brother.) Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"When you're angry, don't let it push you into sin."* (from Ephesians 4:26)

## Chapter Nine
# When I Felt Left Out

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava on the outside of a big window, looking in at a cozy, glowing room full of friends — but Jesus stands beside her on her side of the glass, with his hand gently on her shoulder.

This is a hard one to explain, because nobody did anything MEAN to me. That's what made it so confusing.

There was this birthday party. A big one. With a bounce house.

And basically every girl in my class got invited. They talked about it for a WHOLE WEEK. What they were going to wear. What games there'd be. Who was sitting next to who.

I was not invited.

I don't even think they left me out on purpose. I just wasn't on the list. But knowing it wasn't on purpose did NOT make my heart feel any better.

There's a special kind of lonely where you're surrounded by people and you STILL feel like you're standing on the outside of a window, looking in at a warm room you're not in. That's how I felt at recess, standing there, pretending to be super interested in a rock on the ground.

When I got home I didn't even want to talk. I just flopped on my bed (you know the move by now).

But then I remembered the thing I learned all the way back when I was scared of the dark: *When it feels like you're alone — ESPECIALLY then — you're not.*

So I whispered it. "Jesus, I feel left out. Will you stay with me?"

And here's the honest truth, because this is an honest book: the party did not magically send me a new invitation. The lonely feeling didn't vanish like a magic trick.

But something BETTER happened. I remembered who I am to Jesus.

I am not on the outside of ANYTHING with him. There is no circle he stands in that I'm not invited into. There is no party in his heart that I'm left out of. With Jesus, I am always on the list. I am always picked. I am always wanted.

And once I remembered that, the recess circle didn't have so much power over me anymore.

Because here's the thing about being left out: it makes you feel small and forgettable, like you don't matter much.

But I've got somebody who thinks I matter SO much that he stays with me every single second of every single day and never once forgets I'm here.

And when you know you're THAT loved, one bounce house seems like a pretty small thing to miss.

(Although. I will admit. Bounce houses look really, really fun. I'm only human.)

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, when I feel left out and forgotten, remind me that I'm always wanted by you. Thank you that with you, I'm never standing outside the window. I'm always on the list. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"I've called you by name. You are mine."* (from Isaiah 43:1)

## Chapter Ten
# The Sick Week

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava as a small lump under a heavy blanket, a barely-touched bowl of soup beside her — and a gentle, glowing figure sitting calmly on the edge of her bed, keeping watch.

There are two kinds of sick.

There's the FUN kind, where you have a little cough, so you get to stay home and watch movies and drink ginger ale and everyone feels bad for you. That kind of sick is honestly kind of a vacation.

And then there's the OTHER kind.

The kind where your whole body aches and the light hurts your eyes and you're so tired you can't even finish your soup. The kind where you lose track of which day it is. That kind of sick is not a vacation. That kind of sick is the pits.

I had the bad kind one winter.

Fun fact: my dad actually wrote a book about being sick. It's called *The Sick Seven*. So when I got sick, he joked that I'd become the eighth one. I did not laugh, because laughing hurt. But I did smile a tiny bit, which is all I had the energy for.

Here's the thing nobody warns you about being really sick: it's LONELY. Everyone's being nice to you, bringing you water, feeling your forehead — but YOU are still the only one stuck inside the achy body. Nobody can be sick FOR you. You're kind of alone in there.

One night, when I felt the very worst, my mom sat next to my bed and held my hand. I didn't have the energy to even talk. But she said something quiet that I keep with me.

She said: "You know, when Jesus was here, he spent SO much of his time with sick people. Everybody else stayed away from them — they were scared of catching something. But not Jesus. He'd walk right up close. He'd put his hand on the people nobody else would even touch. He LIKED being near the ones who felt the worst."

I thought about that, lying there feeling like the worst.

"So he's not far away right now?" I whispered. "Even though I'm all gross and sick?"

"ESPECIALLY right now," my mom said. "This is exactly the kind of bedside he loves to sit at."

So I closed my eyes and imagined him there. On the other side of the bed from my mom. Not scared of my fever. Not waiting for me to get better and cuter and less gross. Just sitting close, the way he always has.

I got better that week. I always do. The soup started tasting good again, the light stopped stabbing my eyes, and one morning I woke up and I was just ME again.

But I didn't forget the Sick Week. Because that's the week I learned that Jesus isn't only with me on my good days, when I'm healthy and grinning at the baseball field in my gold shoes.

He's with me on my worst day. My grossest, achiest, can't-finish-the-soup day.

He sits at that bedside too.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, thank you that you come CLOSE to people when they feel their worst. Thank you for sitting at my bedside on the sick days and the hard days. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"It's not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick."* (from Mark 2:17)

## Chapter Eleven
# Praying for Somebody Else (a Plot Twist)

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** A cutaway of the house so you can see two rooms at once: Ava praying at her bedside in one room, and a soft glowing line traveling across the house to her grandma resting in another room.

So far in this book, I'll be honest, most of my prayers have been about... me.

Help ME not be scared. Help ME beat my brother. Give ME a dog named Waffles. Help ME feel better. Me, me, me.

And that's okay! Jesus WANTS me to bring him my stuff. But one day something happened that taught me prayer has a whole other setting I didn't even know about.

It was when my grandma got sick. (Same grandma from the Waffles chapter.) Real sick. The kind where the grown-ups talk in quiet voices in the kitchen and stop when you walk in.

I felt SO helpless. I was just a kid. I couldn't fix her. I couldn't make medicine. I couldn't do ANYTHING.

And then I realized — wait. There IS one thing I can do. The same thing I'd been doing for myself this whole time.

I can pray. But this time, not for me. For HER.

So every night I prayed for my grandma. "Jesus, please be with Grandma. Help her not be scared. Sit at her bedside like you sat at mine in the Sick Week. And help the doctors. And help my mom, because she's scared too, even though she pretends she's not."

And something surprised me. Praying for somebody ELSE felt different than praying for myself. When I prayed for me, it made me feel better. But when I prayed for Grandma, it made me feel... bigger. Like my small kid-sized self had actually done something real. Like I'd reached out across the whole house and put my hand on her, even from my own bed.

My grandma got a little better, and then she got to go home. I don't know exactly how much my prayers did. That's not really for me to know. But I know they did SOMETHING, because they changed ME. They turned me from a helpless little kid into somebody who could actually help.

That's when I learned the secret about praying for other people: you can't always fix somebody with your own two hands. But you can ALWAYS carry them to Jesus. And carrying somebody to Jesus is one of the most powerful things a regular kid can do.

So now my prayers aren't only about me anymore.

Well. Mostly. I still pray for the dog sometimes. Operation Waffles never fully ends.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, help me remember to pray for OTHER people, not just myself. When someone I love is hurting and I can't fix it, let me carry them to you. That's something even a kid can do. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Pray for each other."* (from James 5:16)

## Chapter Twelve
# How I Actually Talk to Him

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava praying while hanging completely upside-down off the side of her bed, hair dangling toward the floor, a peaceful smile on her face.

Okay. By now you've heard me say "and then I prayed" about a hundred times. So you might be sitting there thinking: *Ava. HOW? I can't see Jesus. He doesn't talk back out loud. So how do you actually DO it?*

Great question. I wondered the same thing for a long time. So here are the official instructions, from me to you.

**Instruction #1: It's just talking.**

That's it. That's the whole secret. Praying is just talking to Jesus like he's right there. Because he is. You don't need fancy words. You don't need to sound like a grown-up at church. You can just talk normal. You can even tell him about the block dog.

**Instruction #2: You don't have to do anything special with your body.**

People think you have to close your eyes and fold your hands and kneel down. You CAN if you want! I usually close my eyes, but only because it helps me not get distracted by, like, a cool spider on the ceiling. But you can pray with your eyes open, walking around, or even hanging upside-down off the side of your bed. Jesus doesn't care about the position. He cares that you showed up.

**Instruction #3: You can tell him ANYTHING.**

The good stuff, the embarrassing stuff, the stuff you wouldn't tell another living soul. The mad stuff (see: my brother). The sad stuff. The thank-you stuff. He's not going to be shocked. He already knows. He just likes when you bring it to him instead of carrying it alone.

**But Ava,** you might say, **does he ever answer back?**

Yes. But almost never in a big booming voice from the sky. (That's never happened to me. Not once.) He answers in QUIET ways.

Like — sometimes I'm worried, and after I talk to him, the worry gets smaller. That's an answer. Sometimes I'm scared to do the kind thing, and a brave feeling shows up that wasn't there before. That's an answer. Sometimes the answer is just a deep-down knowing that I'm not alone and everything's going to be okay.

You learn to notice him the way you learn to notice anything. At first you don't see it. Then once you know what to look for, you see it everywhere.

So that's the whole instruction manual. Easy, right?

You just have to start. Tonight, in your own bed, in your own dark, you can say the same exact thing I said when I was four:

"Jesus, will you stay with me?"

And he will. He already is.

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, thank you that I can talk to you anytime, anywhere, in any position, about anything, with no fancy words. Help me get quiet enough to notice the ways you answer. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"Ask, and it will be given to you. Knock, and the door will open."* (from Matthew 7:7)

## Chapter Thirteen
# Now I Am Nine

> 🎨 **Chapter picture:** Ava sitting up on her familiar yellow rail at the baseball field at golden hour — and now, sitting right beside her, you can faintly see a gentle glowing presence. She was never up there alone.

Well. Here we are. The last chapter.

If you read this whole book, first of all: WOW. Thank you. You stuck with me through the chess disaster, the deflated-balloon days, the block-town crime, and Operation Waffles. You're basically my friend now. Those are the rules.

So let me tell you what I figured out by writing all of this down.

I started this book as a kind of diary. Just a bunch of separate stories. The dark. The sad days. My brother. The bounce house. The Sick Week. Grandma.

But when I put them all in a row like this, I noticed something I COULDN'T see when I was living them one at a time.

He was in every single one.

He was there in the dark when I was scared.
He was there on the gray balloon days.
He was there at the chessboard when I lost (again).
He was there cooling me down before I bonked my brother.
He was there outside the bounce-house window.
He was there at my bedside in the Sick Week.
He was there when I prayed for Grandma.
He's here RIGHT NOW, while I write this, in my own room.

My whole life — the whole time — in every single chapter — he was with me.

That's not a small thing. That's the biggest thing I know.

And here's the part that makes me happiest of all:

The book is ending. But the story is NOT.

I'm only just turning nine! I've got so much life left. There will be more new schools, more goodbyes, more chess losses, more gray days, more sick weeks. (Probably more block-town crimes, honestly. My brother has big feet.) But there will ALSO be a thousand more golden evenings. More best days than I can count.

And I already know the one thing that'll be true in every single one of them — the good, the bad, and the in-between:

He'll be with me.

When I'm ten, and twelve, and a teenager, and a grown-up, and OLD. When I'm somewhere I can't even imagine yet. When I'm scared, or sad, or so happy my heart could tip over.

He'll be there. He'll already be there, getting there before me, the way he always does.

So I don't have to be scared of any of it.

Because I learned the secret of my whole entire life:

**I am never, ever walking in by myself.**

He is with me.

And — hey, you, reading this —

He is with you too.

*The End. (But really... the beginning.)*

> 💛 **A little prayer:** *Jesus, thank you for being with me my whole life, in every single chapter. Thank you that you'll be in all the chapters still to come. I'm so glad I'm never alone. I love you. Amen.*

> 📖 **Jesus says:** *"And for sure, I am with you always — to the very end."* (Matthew 28:20)
