# Jesus With Me
### *The story of my whole life, and how Jesus was always there* — by Ava

A children's chapter book for ages 8–10, told in the first-person voice of a
girl named Ava (turning nine). It is **not** a Bible retelling — it is *Ava's
own story*: each chapter is a different time in her life, from before she can
remember up to right now, and how Jesus was with her in every one.

The arc is her whole life: baby → afraid of the dark → first day of school →
a friend moving away → the day she was unkind → feeling left out → a sick week
→ a perfect golden day → learning how to pray → turning nine and looking ahead.
Hard chapters and happy chapters on purpose, because Jesus is in both.

## How this folder is organized

- `chapters/` — one Markdown file per chapter, in reading order. These are the
  source of truth; edit these with Ava.
- `jesus-with-me-FULL.md` — the whole book stitched into one file for reading,
  printing, or sending to an illustrator. (Regenerate it after edits — see below.)

### To rebuild the full manuscript after editing chapters
From inside the `jesus-with-me/` folder:
```
cat chapters/*.md > jesus-with-me-FULL.md
```

## Each chapter's shape (so new ones stay consistent)

1. A short true-feeling story from a moment in Ava's life.
2. A turn where a parent (or Ava herself) names where Jesus was in it.
3. A gentle landing — what Ava learned, in her own words.
4. **A little prayer** — one or two lines in Ava's voice.
5. **Jesus says** — a short, kid-friendly paraphrase of a real verse, with the
   reference, so it can be looked up in a real Bible.

## Notes for an illustrator

Ava (the narrator and main character): a happy nine-year-old girl with long,
wavy brown hair, a big warm smile, often in a red/pink heart shirt, jeans, and
shiny **gold sneakers**. A recurring setting is a **baseball field at golden
hour** with a long, round **yellow rail** she likes to sit on (mountains in the
distance) — this is the scene of Chapter 8 and a good cover candidate.

Suggested feel: warm, soft, golden light. Jesus is mostly *felt* rather than
drawn — consider showing him as a gentle presence (a warmth, a hand, a figure
just at the edge) rather than a literal face, so each child can picture him.

The book ends with an invitation for the reader to draw their *own* first
chapter — leave a few blank illustration pages at the back.
