# Jesus With Me

### The story of my whole life, and how Jesus was always there

*by Ava*

*with help from Dad*

---

*For every kid who ever wondered if they were alone.*
*You never were.*

---

> "And surely I am with you always,
> to the very end of the age."
> — Jesus (Matthew 28:20)
# Chapter One: Before I Can Remember

My name is Ava, and I am almost nine years old.

That is old enough to know a lot of things. I know how to tie my shoes with two loops. I know that the moon follows the car. I know that if you eat the crust, you get curly hair, which is probably not true, but I eat it anyway just in case.

But there is one thing I know best of all, and it took me my whole life to learn it.

Jesus has been with me the entire time.

I cannot remember being a baby. Nobody can. But my mom told me about it, and she keeps the pictures in a blue box on the shelf. There is one of me when I was brand new, smaller than a loaf of bread, wrapped up tight like a little burrito. My eyes are squeezed shut. My hands are tiny.

"You cried all night," my mom said, smiling like it was a happy thing, which is strange.

I asked her if I was alone in the dark, back then, when I was too little to know anything at all.

She said, "No, baby. You were never alone. Not for one second."

And here is the part I love.

Even before I knew the word *Jesus*, before I could talk, before I could even hold up my own head — he was already there. Watching me sleep. Counting my tiny fingers. Loving me before I could love him back.

That is the thing about Jesus. You do not have to be big enough, or smart enough, or good enough. He does not wait for you to catch up. He is just *there*, from the very first day, like the sky was there before you ever looked up at it.

I think that is the most amazing thing I have ever heard.

So this book is the story of my whole life. All the way from the blue box of baby pictures, up to right now, almost nine. And in every single part, even the parts I cannot remember, Jesus was with me.

Let me show you.

# Chapter Two: When I Was Afraid of the Dark

When I was four, I was afraid of the dark.

Not a little afraid. A LOT afraid. The kind of afraid where you do not even want to put your foot off the bed, because who knows what is under there.

At night my room turned into a different room. In the daytime it was just my room, with my books and my socks on the floor. But when the light went out, the closet looked like a cave, and my coat on the hook looked like a tall, skinny man standing very still.

I would call out, "MOM! DAD!" And one of them would come, and turn on the light, and the tall skinny man would turn back into my coat, like he was never anything at all.

But then they would leave, and the dark would come back.

One night my dad sat on the edge of my bed. The light was off, but I could see the shape of him, big and safe.

"Ava," he said, "do you want to know a secret about the dark?"

I did.

"Jesus is not afraid of it," he said. "Not even a little. The dark is nothing to him. He made the light *and* the dark, and he can see in both the same. So when it is dark, and you cannot see anything, you can talk to him, and he is right there. He never closes his eyes. He is never asleep."

I thought about that.

"So when it is dark," I said slowly, "and I am scared... I am not by myself?"

"You are never by yourself," my dad said. "Even when it feels like it. *Especially* when it feels like it."

That night, after he left, the dark came back like it always did. The closet got cave-ish. The coat got tall.

But this time I did something new. I did not call out. I just whispered, very quiet, "Jesus, you can see in the dark. Will you stay with me?"

And here is the truth. The dark did not change. The room was just as black.

But *I* changed. The scared feeling got smaller and smaller, like a balloon letting out its air, until it was just a little thing I could hold in my hand. And then I was asleep.

I am almost nine now, and I am not really afraid of the dark anymore. But I still talk to Jesus at night. Not because I am scared. Just because he is there, and it would be silly not to talk to someone who is right there.

The dark was never the thing that kept me safe.

He was.

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, you can see in the dark, and the dark is nothing to you. When I am scared, help me remember you are right here. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"Do not be afraid, for I am with you." (from Isaiah 41:10)*
# Chapter Three: My First Day

When I was five, I started kindergarten.

I had been waiting my WHOLE life for this. Well, five years, which was my whole life. I had a backpack with a unicorn on it. I had brand new shoes. I was ready.

And then the morning came, and I was not ready at all.

It is a funny thing. You can want something so much, and then the moment it is real, your stomach gets all wobbly and you change your mind completely. That is how I felt standing in front of the school. It was so big. There were so many kids. They all seemed to know exactly where to go, and I did not know anything.

My mom knelt down so we were nose to nose.

"You are going to have a wonderful day," she said.

"What if nobody likes me?" I whispered. That was the real fear. Not the bigness of the school. The bigness of being alone in it.

My mom smiled. "Ava, you are not walking in there by yourself. You know that, right?"

"You can't come in with me," I said. "Only kids."

"Not me," she said. "Jesus. He goes everywhere you go. He will be there when you find your seat. He will be there at lunch. He will be there at recess, even if you are standing by yourself for a minute. You are never the only one in the room."

So I walked in.

And it was scary for about four minutes. Then a girl named Lucy showed me where the cubbies were, and asked if I wanted to be partners, and that was that.

But here is the thing I figured out later, when I was bigger and could think about it.

It was not just that I made a friend. It was that even in the four scary minutes *before* the friend — even when I was standing there alone with my unicorn backpack and my wobbly stomach — I was not actually alone. Jesus was already in the room. He got there before me. He always gets there before me.

Every new thing in my life has been a little like that first day of school. A new team. A new class. A new place where I do not know anybody yet.

And every single time, I have to remember the same thing my mom told me when I was five.

I am not walking in by myself.

He is already inside.

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, thank you for going ahead of me into all the new and scary places. Help me remember you are already there before I even arrive. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"I will never leave you nor forsake you." (from Hebrews 13:5)*
# Chapter Four: When My Friend Moved Away

When I was six, my best friend moved away.

Her name was Lucy. Yes, the same Lucy from the cubbies on my first day of school. We had been best friends for a whole year, which when you are six is basically forever.

Then one day she came to school with a sad face and said her family was moving to a different state. A faraway one. The kind you cannot ride a bike to.

I did not really understand it until the day the big truck came. I watched the men carry Lucy's couch out the front door, and her bed, and the little table where we used to color. The truck swallowed up her whole house, piece by piece, until it was empty.

Then Lucy hugged me, and got in the car, and the car got smaller and smaller down the street until it turned a corner and was gone.

I cried so hard I got the hiccups.

"It is not fair," I told my dad. "She was my best friend and now she is just *gone*. Everybody leaves."

My dad did not tell me to stop crying. He did not say it was no big deal. He just sat with me on the front step while I had my hiccups.

After a while he said, "Ava, can I tell you something true? People do leave sometimes. That part really is sad, and you are allowed to be sad about it. I am sad too. But there is one friend who never moves away. Not to a different state. Not ever. No truck is big enough to carry him off."

I knew who he meant.

"Jesus does not pack up and leave," my dad said. "When everybody else goes, he stays. He is the friend who is still there on the empty front step with you. He is here right now."

I looked around the front step. I could not see anybody but my dad.

But I sort of *felt* it. That I was not as alone as I thought. That even with Lucy gone and the house empty, there was somebody still sitting close.

I still miss Lucy. We send each other drawings in the mail sometimes. But I learned something on that step that I did not know before.

People can be best friends, and best friends are wonderful, and you should love them as much as you can.

But people can move away.

Jesus stays.

---

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, when people I love go away, it makes me sad. Thank you that you never move away. Thank you for staying. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"I am with you always, to the very end." (from Matthew 28:20)*
# Chapter Five: The Day I Was Mean

When I was seven, I said something mean.

I do not even like writing this chapter, because I am not proud of it. But my dad says the true stories are the ones worth telling, even the parts that make your face feel hot. So here it is.

There was a girl in my class named Priya. One day, in front of everybody, I said something mean about her drawing. I do not want to write exactly what I said. It was the kind of thing you say to get the other kids to laugh.

And they did laugh.

But Priya did not laugh. Her face went red and her eyes got shiny, and she put her drawing in her desk so nobody could see it anymore.

The laughing felt good for about one second. Then it felt terrible. It felt terrible for the whole rest of the day.

That night I could not sleep. The mean thing I said kept playing over and over in my head, like a song I did not want stuck in there. I felt like the worst person in the world. I thought, *Jesus must be so disappointed in me. He probably does not even want to be near me right now.*

That was the scariest thought of all. Worse than the dark. The thought that I had done something so bad that Jesus would back away from me.

I told my mom. I cried and told her the whole thing, even the part that made my face hot.

And my mom said something I will never forget.

"Ava," she said, "Jesus did not back away from you tonight. He came closer. That terrible feeling you have? That is not him leaving. That is him *with* you, helping you see that you want to make it right. People who do not care feel nothing. You feel awful because your heart is good and it knows it did a wrong thing."

"But what do I do?" I said. "I cannot un-say it."

"No," she said. "But you can say sorry. And here is the best news in the whole world. When you do something wrong and you bring it to Jesus, he does not keep a list. He forgives you completely. Clean. Like fresh snow. And then he helps you be braver next time."

The next day was hard. I went up to Priya and I said I was sorry, and that her drawing was actually really good, which was true. My voice was shaky. She did not forgive me right away, and that was fair. But after a few days, she did. And now she is one of my good friends.

Here is what I learned, and it is maybe the most important thing in this whole book.

Jesus does not only stay with me when I am good.

He stays with me when I mess up, too. He stays close enough to help me fix it.

That is a friend who really, truly never leaves.

---

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, I am sorry for the times I am unkind. Thank you that you forgive me completely and help me make things right. Make my heart more like yours. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"Neither do I condemn you. Go now, and be kind." (from John 8:11)*
# Chapter Six: When I Felt Left Out

When I was eight, I felt left out.

This one is hard to explain, because nobody did anything mean to me. That is what made it so confusing. Nobody pushed me or said a bad word. It was quieter than that.

There was a group of girls who all got invited to a birthday party. A big one, with a bounce house. They talked about it at school for a whole week — what they would wear, what games there would be, who was sitting by who.

I was not invited.

I do not think they left me out on purpose. I just was not on the list. But knowing it was not on purpose did not make my heart feel any better. At recess they all stood in a circle talking about the party, and I stood a little ways away, pretending to be very interested in a rock.

I have never felt so left out in my life. It is a special kind of lonely, the kind where you are surrounded by people and still feel like you are standing on the outside of a window, looking in.

When I got home I did not even want to talk about it. I just laid on my bed.

But I remembered something. All the way back from when I was four and scared of the dark. *When it feels like you are by yourself, especially then, you are not.*

So I whispered it, the way I learned a long time ago. "Jesus, I feel left out. Will you stay with me?"

And I want to tell you the honest truth, because this is a true book.

The party did not magically get a new invitation for me. The lonely feeling did not vanish like a magic trick.

But something happened that was even better than that. I remembered who I am to Jesus. I am not on the *outside* of anything with him. There is no circle he stands in that I am not invited to. There is no party in his heart that I am left out of. With Jesus, I am always on the list. I am always picked. I am always wanted.

And once I remembered that, the recess circle did not have so much power over me anymore.

The thing about being left out is that it makes you feel small and forgettable, like you do not matter very much.

But I have somebody who thinks I matter so much that he stays with me every single second of every single day, and never once forgets I am here.

When you know you are *that* loved, a bounce house seems like a pretty small thing to miss.

(Though I will admit, bounce houses are really fun. I am only human.)

---

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, when I feel left out and forgotten, remind me that I am always wanted by you. Thank you that with you I am never on the outside. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"I have called you by name; you are mine." (from Isaiah 43:1)*
# Chapter Seven: The Sick Week

There was one winter when I got really, truly sick.

Not the kind of sick where you get to stay home and watch movies and it is secretly a little bit fun. The other kind. The kind where your whole body aches and the light hurts your eyes and you cannot even finish your soup.

My dad writes books, and one of his books is about being sick — it is called *The Sick Seven*. So when I got sick, he joked that I had become the eighth one. I did not laugh, because laughing hurt, but I did smile a tiny bit.

The days went by slow and blurry. I lost track of which day was which. I just stayed in my bed, under the heavy blanket, feeling small.

Being sick is lonely in a way I did not expect. Everyone is being nice to you, bringing you water and feeling your forehead, but you are still the only one who has to be inside the sick body. Nobody can do it for you. You are kind of alone in there.

One night, when I felt the very worst, my mom sat next to my bed and held my hand. I did not have the energy to talk. But she said something quietly.

"You know," she said, "when Jesus was here, he spent so much of his time with sick people. Everybody else stayed away from them, because they were afraid of catching something. But Jesus walked right up close. He was not scared of sick. He would put his hand right on people that nobody else would touch. He liked being near the ones who felt the worst."

I thought about that, lying there feeling like the worst.

"So he is not far away right now," I whispered. "Even though I am all gross and sick."

"Especially right now," my mom said. "This is exactly the kind of bedside he loves to sit at."

I closed my eyes. And I imagined him there, on the other side of the bed from my mom. Not scared of my fever. Not waiting for me to get better and prettier and less gross. Just sitting close in the dark, the way he had since I was four years old, the way he always has.

I got better that week. I always do. The soup started tasting good again, and the light stopped hurting, and one morning I woke up and I was just *me* again.

But I did not forget the sick week. Because that was the week I learned that Jesus is not only with me on my good days, when I am healthy and smiling at the baseball field in my gold shoes.

He is with me on my worst day. My grossest, achiest, soup-can't-finish day.

He sits at that bedside too.

---

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, thank you that you come close to people when they feel their worst. Thank you for sitting with me on my sick days and my hard days. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick." (from Mark 2:17)*
# Chapter Eight: The Best Day

Not all of my chapters are about hard things. I want to tell you about a good day, because Jesus is there for those too. Maybe people forget that part. They remember to pray when they are scared or sad. But Jesus is just as close on the best day of your life as on the worst.

So here is one of my best days.

It was a warm evening at the baseball field. The big lights were not even on yet, because the sun was still going down behind the hills, turning everything gold and pink. I was wearing my favorite heart shirt and my gold sneakers, the ones that catch the light.

I climbed up and sat on the long yellow rail by the field, the round bumpy one, with my feet hanging down. From up there you can see the whole field, the green grass and the dirt diamond and the mountains way far off.

I do not even remember what was so special about it. Nothing big happened. My family was there. The grass smelled like summer. Somebody was laughing. I had that feeling you get sometimes, when everything is just *right*, and your heart is so full it might tip over.

And sitting up on that rail, with my gold shoes shining, I had a thought I had never had before.

*Thank you.*

Not to anybody in particular at first. Just a thank you that bubbled up out of me because the evening was so beautiful. And then I knew exactly who I was thanking.

I was thanking Jesus. Because all of it — the gold light, the green field, the laughing, the warm air, the heart so full it might tip over — all of it came from him. He did not just make the hard days and then show up to help me through them. He made the *good* days too. The best ones. The golden ones. He made the whole beautiful world and then put me right in the middle of it to enjoy it.

I used to think you mostly needed Jesus for the scary parts. The dark, the sick week, the day I was mean, the day I felt left out. Like he was an umbrella you only take out when it rains.

But that golden evening on the yellow rail, I understood something more.

He is not just the umbrella for the rain.

He is the one who made the sunshine, too.

And on the best day of your life, when your heart is so full it might tip over, he is right there next to you on the rail, watching the sun go down, just as happy as you are.

---

**A little prayer:**
*Jesus, thank you for the good days, the golden ones, the ones so beautiful my heart could tip over. Thank you for being there for those too. Amen.*

**Jesus says:**
*"I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full." (from John 10:10)*
# Chapter Nine: How I Talk to Him

By now you might be wondering something. You might be thinking: *Ava, you keep saying you talk to Jesus. But how? You cannot see him. He does not talk back out loud. So how do you actually do it?*

That is a good question. I wondered it too for a long time. So let me tell you how I figured it out, because it is easier than you think.

Talking to Jesus is called praying. But do not let the fancy word scare you. Praying is just talking. You do not have to use special words. You do not have to close your eyes, though I usually do because it helps me think. You do not have to kneel down or fold your hands a certain way, though you can if you want.

You just... talk to him. Like he is right there. Because he is.

I do most of my talking at night, in bed, in the dark. The same dark that used to scare me when I was four. Funny how that works. The thing I was once most afraid of turned into my favorite quiet time with him.

Here is what I do. I tell him about my day. The whole thing. The good parts and the embarrassing parts and the parts I would not tell anybody else. I tell him who I am worried about. I tell him thank you for stuff. Sometimes I just lie there and do not say anything at all, and that is praying too, the same way you can sit with your best friend and not talk and it is still nice.

"But Ava," you might say, "does he ever answer?"

Yes. But not usually in a big booming voice from the sky. That has never happened to me. He answers in quieter ways. Sometimes I am worried about something, and after I talk to him about it, the worry gets smaller, like the balloon letting out its air. That is an answer. Sometimes I am trying to decide if I should do something kind that feels scary, and a brave feeling shows up that was not there before. That is an answer. Sometimes the answer is just a deep-down knowing that I am not alone, and that everything is going to be okay.

You learn to notice him, the way you learn to notice anything. At first you do not see it. Then once you know what to look for, you see it everywhere.

So that is the secret, if it even is a secret.

You do not need anything special to talk to Jesus.

You just need to start. Tonight, in your own bed, in your own dark, you can say the same thing I said when I was four years old.

"Jesus, will you stay with me?"

And he will. He already is.

# Chapter Ten: Now I Am Nine

And now we are all the way here. Now I am almost nine.

We started in the blue box of baby pictures, with me wrapped up like a little burrito, too small to know anything at all. And we came all the way up, year by year, to right now, to me writing this book.

When I look back at my whole life so far, I can see something I could not see when I was little. I can see it now because I am big enough to look back at all of it at once.

In every single chapter, he was there.

He was there before I can remember, counting my tiny fingers.
He was there in the dark when I was four.
He was there on my first day of school when I was five.
He was there on the empty front step when Lucy moved away.
He was there the night I felt like the worst person in the world, and he came *closer*, not farther.
He was there at recess when I felt left out and forgotten.
He was there at my bedside on the worst day of the sick week.
He was there on the golden evening on the yellow rail, just as happy as I was.
He is here right now, while I write this, in my own room, in my own quiet.

My whole life, the whole time, in every part — he was with me.

That is not a small thing. That is the biggest thing I know.

And here is what makes me the happiest of all. The book is ending, but the story is not. I am only almost nine. I have so much more life to go. There will be more new schools, and more goodbyes, and more days I mess up, and more times I feel left out, and more sick weeks. But there will also be a thousand more golden evenings. More best days than I can count.

And I already know the one thing that will be true in every single one of them, the good and the hard and the in-between.

He will be with me.

When I am ten, and twelve, and a teenager, and a grown-up, and old. When I am someplace I cannot even imagine yet. When I am scared, or sad, or so happy my heart could tip over.

He will be there. He will already be there, getting there before me, the way he always does.

I do not have to be afraid of any of it.

Because I have learned the secret of my whole life.

I am never, ever walking in by myself.

He is with me.

He is with you, too.

*The End — but really, the beginning.*

# A Note for Whoever Is Reading This

Hi. It's me, Ava.

If you read all the way to here, thank you. I hope you liked my story.

I wrote this book because I wanted other kids to know what I know. That you are not alone. Not ever. Not in the dark, not on your first day, not when your friend moves away, not when you mess up, not when you feel left out, not even when you are sick in bed feeling like the worst.

Jesus has been with me my whole life, all the way from the baby pictures up to now.

And he wants to be with you the same way.

You do not have to be big enough, or good enough, or smart enough. I am only almost nine, and I am not any of those things all the way yet. He does not wait for you to catch up. He is just there, already, like the sky.

So here is what I want to tell you, the same thing I have said since I was four years old in the dark:

You can talk to him. Tonight. In your own bed. You can say,

*"Jesus, will you stay with me?"*

And he will.

He already is.

Love,
Ava
